3 · What we plan to do

Five calls we're making for the build

Informed by what attorneys told us. Paired with our take on what works. Each one ties back to a mandate objective. Click any card for the longer reasoning.

Build inside Outlook only

Obj 1, 4
Categories, flags, and Microsoft To Do as the surface. No add-in dependency for anything that needs to work on mobile.
Why. 4 of 9 attorneys cannot use the add-in pane on mobile. 3 are on Apple Mail. 1 is on Android. Categories and flags propagate to all of them through Exchange. Mandate: triage efficiency, attorney trust.

Make sender a primary feature in the model

Obj 1
The model treats sender identity as a top-level input alongside subject and content. Per-sender pattern learning from day one.
Why. 8 of 9 attorneys triage sender-first. The model has to align with how attorneys already think. Mandate: triage efficiency.

Keep every action attorney-initiated

Obj 3, 4
Filing, archive, deletion, response. Every one needs an attorney's click before it happens.
Why. 9 of 9 attorneys converged on this when asked about guardrails. The principle is in the SOW and empirically validated. Mandate: filing consistency, attorney trust.

Make AI behavior configurable per attorney

Obj 1, 4
Default settings plus opt-up and opt-down. Cadence, category aggressiveness, and notification thresholds tunable per attorney.
Why. 3 distinct AI postures across 7 active users. Plus 5 different digest cadence preferences. One default won't serve everyone. Mandate: triage efficiency, attorney trust.

Support per-matter exclusions in the engine

Obj 4
Attorneys can opt specific matters, clients, or categories out of AI processing entirely. The exclusion is honored at the engine level, not as a post-hoc filter.
Why. Mills surfaced one matter with this need. The same concern likely applies in employment, government investigation, and high-profile work. The architecture has to support it from Phase 2. Mandate: attorney trust.
4 · Where we need you

Five questions for the workshops ahead

Questions that need your input before architecture is locked.

On owners and timelines. We'll lock owners and dates as we hear who's best placed to lead each thread. Today is about getting the questions on the table and confirming we're solving the right ones.
Q1
When an attorney has a matter so confidential that AI access raises real concerns, what's the right way to opt that thread out of categorization — at the matter level, the client level, or by category?
Our thinking
Per-matter exclusion as the default, with a per-category fallback for sensitive practice areas like government investigations and personnel work. The exclusion is honored at the engine level, so the model never sees the content at all.
Q2
Some attorneys' Legal Administrative Assistants (LAAs) have access to their calendar but not their inbox — Brusven, for example, draws this wall because of EC-sensitive email. Does the system's permission model need to honor that partition, and which other attorneys share it?
Our thinking
Yes, the architecture has to handle calendar-yes / inbox-no. Snelson, Cassioppi, and Yevzelman all sit on EC or Board and likely share the pattern, but it hasn't been verified. Phase 1 architecture workshop with Brent confirms.
Q3
The discovery cohort is shareholders only — no IP attorney and no associate, both of whom we expect to behave differently from what we've heard. Who should we add to the pilot, and when?
Our thinking
One IP attorney and one associate added before architecture is locked, with brief delta-discovery sessions to surface practice-specific signals (docketing patterns for IP, supervisor-as-client dynamics for associates).
Q4
Five different cadence preferences came out of discovery — real-time nudges, mid-day, end-of-day, no-nudges, weekly. Should we launch with one default and tune from there, or stagger by attorney from day one?
Our thinking
End-of-day default at launch. Per-attorney cadence preference surfaced in the first tuning conversation. Simpler to launch, easier to measure baseline, gives attorneys an immediate visible decision point.
Q5
Three attorneys are on Apple Mail iOS. Categories, flags, and To Do should propagate through Exchange, but the propagation behavior in F&B's specific tenant has never been tested end-to-end. Can we run a quick technical validation before architecture is locked?
Our thinking
Brent runs a tenant-side test: create a category, apply to a test email, confirm it appears on Apple Mail iOS. Same for flag and To Do. If propagation fails, the design adjusts before Phase 2 starts.

Universal pattern · 8 of 9

Sender is the dominant triage signal

Why it matters for the build

Categorization that reasons primarily from email content will feel foreign to attorneys. The model has to align with how attorneys already think.

Per-sender pattern learning is the highest-leverage capability. Brusven's mental "I know Jared's emails go to this client" mapping is exactly what the system should learn automatically.

How it showed up

Mills, Norah, Snelson, Brusven, Yevzelman, Cassioppi, Hatcher, Z. Olson all triage by sender first. Maddy implied through her search behavior.

Z. Olson built Outlook color rules to highlight sole-recipient and key-sender emails. He engineered his own version of sender-first triage because he needed it for two large West Coast sponsor clients driving heavy deal volume.

Universal pattern · 9 of 9

Email is the work surface

Why it matters for the build

No new aggregation surfaces. No standalone client app. No panels that compete with the inbox. The tool meets attorneys where they already are.

This is also the basis for resisting any future scope expansion that would move attorneys out of Outlook.

The clearest articulation
If that has to be like over in Teams, we're gonna lose a bunch of people because they won't go over there and look.Norah
How it showed up

Every attorney described email as the place their work lives. Where it's queued, prioritized, started, and frequently finished. Nobody said anything resembling "I move things out of email and work somewhere else."

Snelson at 60,000 inbox items. Cassioppi at 105,000. Brusven at 9,400. These aren't unmanaged piles. They're the attorneys' work surface.

Universal pattern · 9 of 9

Post-meeting overwhelm

Why it matters for the build

The daily digest isn't for the steady-state inbox. It's for the post-meeting reset.

Different scenario. Different design priorities. The digest spec optimizes for "I just got back, what did I miss" rather than "my morning routine."

How attorneys described it

Mills came out of a long hearing to 48 emails and the immediate stress of inbox-zero discipline colliding with reality. Yevzelman: constant bombardment. Zach: 100 emails in an hour as not unusual.

Hatcher described the post-meeting return as a recurring friction point. Maddy heard the same pattern from the attorneys she supports. Important emails buried two-thirds down a 40-email return queue.

Universal pattern · 9 of 9

Filing happens at "done"

Why it matters for the build

The filing trigger is human-judged. Never AI-judged.

The system can detect signals of completion. Unflagging. Replied-and-not-followed-up. No new activity for N days. But the attorney decides.

Norah's anchor
I would never move anything over into a folder until I was done with it. Like I would never have pending work over in the folder.Norah
The variation in "done" semantics

Norah anchored "done" as substantive-work-concluded. Brusven uses unflagging as her trigger and proposed an unflag-and-file prompt as her single most valuable feature.

Cassioppi: "done is it's been addressed." A looser bar. Maddy treats filing as separate from completion entirely. The semantics vary. The principle is identical.

Universal pattern · 8 of 9

Mobile triage core, mobile filing broken

Why it matters for the build

The filing UI is desktop-anchored. Mobile filing where it works is a bonus, not a baseline.

Categories, flags, and To Do propagate across every device through Exchange. That keeps the mobile experience coherent without depending on an add-in that 4 of 9 attorneys can't access anyway.

Who actually files from mobile

Z. Olson is the only consistent mobile-filer. Outlook on iOS, files cleanly.

Yevzelman tries from Apple Mail when the prediction guesses correctly. Abandons the phone the moment it gets the matter wrong. Snelson files from Outlook iOS but has fat-finger misfile problems.

The other 6 attorneys avoid filing from mobile entirely. Hatcher doesn't trust how she sees email on the small screen. Norah on Apple Mail has no DMS access. Brusven on Android calls it clunky. Cassioppi rarely tries.

Universal pattern · 9 of 9

AI suggests, attorney decides

Why it matters for the build

This is the SOW's governing principle. It's also empirically validated across the cohort.

It's the basis for resisting any future scope expansion that would have AI act on its own. Even pragmatic adopters like Cassioppi tolerate a 10% error rate because they're checking. Not because the system gets to act unilaterally.

Norah's articulation

Cohort variation · 4 love · 2 reject

Conversation threading divides the cohort

Why it matters for the build

Categorization, summarization, and digest features have to work thread-aware but display-agnostic.

Categorization logic operates at both message and thread level. Display preserves the attorney's existing preference. Forcing a thread-collapsed view fails for Hatcher and Snelson. Forcing a flat view fails for Maddy and Yevzelman.

Couldn't live without it (4 of 9)

Maddy: "I would not be able to function without that. If I had to search through for the individual emails on a chain, no." Yevzelman: "I couldn't live without it." Z. Olson called it "super helpful." Mills tries to use it.

Actively dislike it (2 of 9)

Hatcher: "I dislike it. I actually got a new phone recently and it defaulted to conversation threading and it was driving me absolutely crazy." Snelson: "When there's a conversation or multiple emails going on related to the same string, it just throws them all together in one place. And I find that really confusing."

Cohort variation · litigator-relevant

Time-based deadlines have segmented value

Why it matters for the build

Court-emitted deadline surfacing is a litigator feature. Not a universal one.

The Phase 1 workshop on time-based surfacing should be litigator-led, with transactional and regulatory voice present to define what they don't want flooding their digest.

The litigator pattern

Mills wants integration with the digest, plus party-side filtering. Government opposing parties don't drive her work the way private opposing parties do. Sophisticated logic specific to her white-collar regulatory practice.

Norah identified IP attorneys as having a parallel pattern. They file with patent and copyright agencies and have a docketing system analogous to litigation. IP is not yet in the cohort and needs to be added before architecture is locked.

The transactional and regulatory side

Hatcher explicitly declined the time-based reminder concept. "Wouldn't help me." Yevzelman: "No, I don't think so. No." Their deadlines come from regulators or deal timelines or simply aren't email-triggered.

Heading into the build · Brusven

Unflag-and-file prompt

Why it matters for the build

This is the strongest single feature concept the discovery surfaced.

It lands in Mark Complete & File, the SOW's filing flow that captures the moment an attorney signals a thread is done. Brusven's unflag is exactly that signal. The system catches it and offers the filing prompt right there.

What "Mark Complete & File" means

It's the SOW's name for the canonical filing action in the pilot. The attorney clicks a single control that does two things at once. It marks the email as complete (the work is done) and prompts the filing destination (where the email goes in iManage).

The unflag event becomes a second trigger for the same flow. When Brusven removes a flag, the system surfaces the filing prompt. She files in one click instead of going hunting for the email to drag it into a folder.

What Brusven asked for

"It would be really great if when I unflag it, it gives me the option to file it." Twice in her session. She has 9,400 inbox items partly because once a flag comes off, the next step rarely happens. Closing that gap is high-leverage for her specifically and useful for any flag-driven attorney.

Heading into the build · Mills

Court alerts into the digest, with party-side filter

Why it matters for the build

Litigators receive substantial volumes of court-emitted email that contain real deadlines buried in noise. Pulling those signals into the daily digest gives litigators a single place to see what's coming.

The party-side filter is the sophisticated piece. Government opposing parties don't drive Mills' white-collar regulatory work the way private opposing parties do. The categorization needs to know which side of the v. matters.

What this looks like in practice

The system recognizes court alert emails by sender pattern. Extracts the deadline. Filters by case party. Surfaces the relevant ones in Mills' daily digest with backdated reminders configurable per attorney.

Phase 1 rules workshop designs the extraction and filter logic with Mills directly. Once the IP attorney joins the cohort, we validate the same approach against their docketing pattern.

Heading into the build · Z. Olson

AI replicates self-built color rules

Why it matters for the build

Z. Olson built his own categorization layer in Outlook because he couldn't manage his volume without one. The AI's job is to replicate what he built, automatically, for everyone.

This is the strongest validation in the cohort that visual relevance cues earn their keep.

What he built

Conditional formatting rules in Outlook. Green for emails where he is the sole recipient. Italics for emails from a designated key sender. He engineered a pre-AI categorization layer to handle two large West Coast sponsor clients driving heavy deal volume.

What the system delivers

The AI categorizes inbound email using sender, recipient list, subject, and content. The pilot's category indicators surface visually inside Outlook. Z. Olson's manual rules become a benchmark for the AI's accuracy.

Heading into the build · Yevzelman

Show-everything baseline view

Why it matters for the build

Categorization has to be additive only. Never reductive.

Any user can toggle into a chronological-everything view at any time. The AI's category indicators layer on top of the inbox. They don't replace it. They don't hide anything.

What Yevzelman asked for

"I just don't know if focus takes away certain things that I would think are important. I'd rather see it all. But I would like reminders, you know, like that's different where it's like you didn't do something."

Translation: AI nudges and category cues are welcome alongside her email. Anything that hides email from her is not.

Why this is foundational

It's not just Yevzelman. The trust-but-verify cohort (Mills, Brusven, Hatcher) needs persistent visibility into what's been categorized to maintain confidence in the system. Brusven articulated the failure mode directly. She might stop checking a category she trusts and miss items.

Show-everything as a baseline solves both. The skeptic gets the visibility she demands. The verifiers get the audit surface they need.

What the tool will NOT do

Automatic filing

What this means

The system never moves an email into iManage on its own. The AI can detect signals that a thread looks complete (unflagging, replied-and-quiet, no new activity for N days) and surface a filing prompt. Acting on those signals silently is not allowed.

Every filing action touches an attorney's click before it happens.

What the tool will NOT do

Silent matter inference

What this means

The system never silently assigns an email to a client or matter on its own. Suggesting a folder is fine. Saving to one without the attorney's confirmation is not.

Misfiling is the most common pain attorneys raised across discovery. The tool's job is to make the right suggestion easy. The attorney still confirms.

What the tool will NOT do

New surfaces to monitor

What this means

No new tabs, panels, folders, or apps that attorneys have to remember to check. The tool lives inside Outlook's existing surfaces — categories, flags, and Microsoft To Do. If something needs to surface to an attorney, it does so through tools they already see.

Norah's framing: "If it's in Teams, we lose people." The same logic applies to anything else outside Outlook.

What the tool will NOT do

Silent actions of any kind

What this means

AI proposes; the attorney decides. No move, send, archive, delete, or auto-reply happens without an explicit click. This is the SOW's governing principle and 9 of 9 attorneys converged on it independently in discovery.

Even pragmatic adopters like Cassioppi tolerate a 10% error rate because the system is making suggestions he checks. Not because the system gets to act on its own.

What the tool will NOT do

Forced thread collapse

What this means

Some attorneys live in conversation-threaded view (Maddy, Yevzelman, Z. Olson, Mills). Others find threading confusing and explicitly reject it (Hatcher, Snelson). The system never forces either preference. Categorization works at both message and thread level. Display follows whatever the attorney already uses.

What the tool will NOT do

Personal voice replacement

What this means

The firm's voice is a feature of client relationships, not friction to eliminate. We don't generate templated drafts that flatten an attorney's individual voice. If we offer drafting assistance, it preserves the attorney's tone rather than overwriting it.

Maddy raised this as a values-level concern. The pilot holds the line.

What the tool will NOT do

Confidential matters without consent

What this means

Attorneys can opt specific matters, clients, or categories out of AI processing entirely. The exclusion is honored at the engine level — the model never sees the excluded content at all.

Mills surfaced one matter where AI access raised concerns that hadn't been worked through. The exclusion mechanism extends to other practices likely to face the same — government investigations, sensitive personnel work, perception-sensitive clients.

What the tool will NOT do

Data outside F&B's tenant

What this means

All AI processing stays inside F&B's Microsoft 365 tenancy. No firm data is sent to external services, no cross-tenant calls, no third-party LLMs that aren't already covered by F&B's enterprise agreements. Aligns with the SOW's RBAC and Entra ID posture.

Decision 1

Categories, flags, and Microsoft To Do are the surface set

What this means

The pilot's working surface is Outlook's universal tools: color-coded categories, follow-up flags, and Microsoft To Do tasks. These are the things attorneys (and where relevant, their LAAs) will see and act on. We're not building a new pane, tab, or app to monitor.

The tools propagate across every device and every mail client through Exchange — Apple Mail iOS, Android Outlook, iPad, desktop. Same state everywhere. The richer filing UI sits on desktop where 8 of 9 attorneys file anyway.

Decision 2

Starting taxonomy: client work, internal firm work, informational, noise

What "taxonomy" means here

The categorization model needs a top-level structure that buckets every email into one of a few labels. The starting buckets we're working from:

Client work — anything tied to a client matter. Internal firm work — committee, board, hiring, group management. Informational — newsletters, alerts, court bulletins. Noise — everything else.

The Phase 1 rules workshop refines these. Multi-role overload (5 of 9 attorneys carry committee or board work) is why "internal firm work" sits as its own bucket alongside client work, not lumped under "firm/admin."

Decision 3

Sender is a top-level feature in the model

What "feature in the model" means

When the AI categorizes an email, it weighs multiple signals: the sender, the subject line, the body content, who else is on the email, when it arrived, and so on. Each of those is a "feature." We're saying sender weighs heavily — the model treats sender identity as a top-tier signal, not a secondary one.

"Per-sender learning from day one" means the system also learns each attorney's mappings: if Brusven always files emails from Jared into the Acme matter, the model picks up that pattern automatically over time. The mental model attorneys already use becomes the system's model.

Decision 4

Per-attorney configuration is built into the architecture

What's configurable per attorney

Three things, from day one: digest cadence (when the daily digest arrives), category aggressiveness (how confident the model has to be before it surfaces a suggestion), and notification thresholds (what counts as "urgent enough to flag"). Attorneys get a default and can tune up or down.

"Built into the architecture" means we design the per-attorney config layer in Phase 2 rather than waiting until Phase 4 tuning. If we leave it for tuning, the trust-but-verify and visibility-first personas churn before the categorization can prove itself.

Decision 5

Show-everything view available from day one

What this looks like in practice

Any user can flip into a chronological-everything view at any time — full inbox, no filtering, no AI categorization layered on. Categorization is additive: it adds visual cues, prioritization, and digest entries on top of the underlying email. It never hides email.

Yevzelman is the most explicit voice on this, but the trust-but-verify users (Mills, Brusven, Hatcher) share the underlying concern. Show-all is the floor of trust.

Decision 6

Mark Complete & File picks up the unflag event

What "Mark Complete & File" means

It's the explicit, attorney-initiated filing action the SOW commits us to building. When an attorney decides an email is done, they trigger one action and the system handles the file path to iManage with a folder suggestion the attorney confirms. No automatic moves. No silent matter inference.

"Picks up the unflag event" means: when an attorney removes a flag, the system treats that as one of the natural triggers for Mark Complete & File. The file-or-discard prompt surfaces. The attorney decides.

Brusven's discovery ask was the strongest single feature concept the cohort gave us. This is how it lands.

Decision 7

Categorization works thread-aware. Display follows the attorney's preference.

What this means

Categorization logic operates at both the individual message level and the thread (conversation) level. Display follows the attorney's existing preference. If you have threading on, you'll see categories rolled up at the thread level. If you have threading off, you'll see categories per-message.

The system never forces either view. Hatcher and Snelson keep their flat view. Maddy and Yevzelman keep their threaded view. The category indicators work in both.

Decision 8

Weekend backlog cleanup time is tracked as a pilot KPI

What gets measured

Five of nine attorneys spend evening or weekend hours catching up on email — what Brusven called her "Saturday cleanup ritual." Several others described similar patterns.

We track the change in that time per attorney through the pilot, alongside the four SOW success criteria (adoption, triage efficiency, filing consistency, follow-up reduction). If the tool works, this number goes down. Self-reported at intake and exit, with optional check-ins at the midpoint.
